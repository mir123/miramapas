Abya Yala

The commission of the Revista de la Universidad de México for its April 2019 edition called Abya Yala forced me to question some of the rules of cartography. Maps as we know them today have their origin with the need for navigation charts to navigate the seas and to declare ownership of other people's lands on paper. The name of America comes from a cartographer of those times and Abya Yala is the way many indigenous peoples of this continent have begun to call it. So it couldn't be just any map.

One of these cartographic norms is the convention of putting the north at the top of the map. When I got rid of that obligation, I had to ask how and why to orient Abya Yala. The result has three reasons. The most important for me is to subvert the latitudinal hierarchies of a continental mass placed vertically, where what is below seems less important than what is above. That is one of the advantages of the European map, that everyone is more or less at the same level. On the other hand, the map was published on a double page and this position was the best way to take advantage of the space. Finally, I do believe that the continent looks much nicer lying down. After seeing it a lot, I got used to it and then made several more maps with similar orientations.

The territories indicated on the map are those that appear in the issue of the magazine. The projection is Mollweide with its center at the -90 meridian.

In that same issue I published a map on the indigenous population in Mexico and the number of speakers of native languages ​​in those places. It is important to understand what is happening with indigenous languages ​​in Mexico, because they are disappearing. As the Mixe linguist Yásnaya Aguilar said in front of the congress of this country, “Our languages ​​do not die, they are killed. The Mexican State has erased them with its single thought, its single culture, its single State.”
